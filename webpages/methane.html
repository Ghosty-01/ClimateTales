What is Methane?
 Methane (CH4) is a powerful greenhouse gas, and is the second-largest contributor to climate warming after carbon dioxide (CO2). A molecule of methane traps more heat than a molecule of CO2, but methane has a relatively short lifespan of 7 to 12 years in the atmosphere, while CO2 can persist for hundreds of years or more.

What is Source of Methane?
 The largest sources of methane are agriculture, fossil fuels, and decomposition of landfill waste. Natural processes account for 40% of methane emissions, with wetlands being the largest natural source.

Is methane essential?
 Methane is not essential for life but is important for energy production, industrial processes, and contributing to the carbon cycle. While it has significant uses, its environmental impact as a greenhouse gas is a growing concern.

Impacts of Methane
 Methane impacts the environment as a potent greenhouse gas, contributing to global warming and air pollution through ground-level ozone formation. Economically, it's vital for energy production and job creation but poses challenges in agriculture due to livestock emissions. Additionally, melting permafrost releases more methane, accelerating climate change. Managing methane emissions is essential to balance its benefits and mitigate its negative effects on health and the environment.

Effects of Methane
 Methane significantly effects the environment and health by contributing to global warming as a potent greenhouse gas. It leads to ground-level ozone formation, which harms air quality and public health. In agriculture, livestock emissions affect sustainability. Additionally, melting permafrost releases more methane, worsening climate change. While it supports energy production, managing methane emissions is crucial to mitigate its negative effects.

Causes of Methane
 Methane emissions come from several sources, including livestock digestion and manure management in agriculture, decomposition of organic waste in landfills, leaks during natural gas extraction and distribution, natural processes in wetlands, and anaerobic conditions in flooded rice fields. The concentration of methane in the atmosphere has more than doubled over the past 200 years. Scientists estimate that this increase is responsible for 20 to 30% of climate warming since the Industrial Revolution

Solution of CH4
 Solutions to address methane (CH‚ÇÑ) emissions focus on a range of strategies aimed at reduction and capture. Implementing technologies to capture methane from landfills and wastewater treatment plants can significantly lower atmospheric emissions. In agriculture, improving livestock management and manure handling helps reduce emissions. Upgrading natural gas infrastructure to minimize leaks during extraction and transport is also crucial. Restoring wetlands can enhance carbon sequestration while reducing emissions, and employing water management techniques in rice cultivation can lower methane production.
